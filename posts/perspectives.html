Investor Perspectives on AI and the Future of Work

By Mrunal Madinur
6 min read
March 13, 2026

Recently, I was fortunate enough to attend a USC Marshall School of Business speaker panel featuring Michael Jones and Scott Stanford.

For a quick introduction: Michael Jones is the former CEO of MySpace and the co-founder and partner at Science, a consumer-focused venture capital firm. He has backed well-known consumer brands such as Dollar Shave Club and Liquid Death since inception.

Scott Stanford is the former Head of Internet Banking at Goldman Sachs and the co-founder and partner at Acme Capital, a deep-tech-oriented venture capital firm.

Having already been fascinated by the recent wave of “doomerism” articles, sitting in that room didn’t just provide insight. It heightened my genuine fear of the future. The seminar, which revolved almost entirely around AI, opened my eyes to a socially bearish perspective on artificial intelligence. Below are my (slightly disgruntled) key takeaways.

The Bear Case

Although I’ve titled this “The Bear Case,” the speakers’ tone made what I am about to say feel less dramatic and more like reality.

Artificial intelligence will reshape nearly every industry.

We are already experiencing this shift. Log into Bloomberg and you see AI-generated summaries. Log into Handshake and the job search is AI optimized. Glassdoor now surfaces AI encapsulations of job reviews. Everything is being altered by AI, and it will continue to be.

Michael and Scott mentioned the Box CEO recently laying off half of his headcount. They also noted that several large enterprises are already debating cutting headcount due to overhiring — and that is before AI has even fully arrived. Imagine the toll the economy will take when AI-driven layoffs fully come into play.

As AI continues to be invested in and onboarded, headcount will likely be reduced. White collar roles will be among the first to go. The real fear behind this is simple: if the majority of white collar occupations vanish, incomes decline and the backbone of the capitalist economy — consumption — weakens.

They didn’t dive into secondary effects like market equilibrium shifts, price changes, government intervention, or social unrest, but the primary scenario alone is enough to take seriously. It is not exaggerated. It is a plausible, realistic case.

Traditional Careers Are No Longer Safe

At one point, someone in the audience asked the question everyone tiptoes around: “Is investment banking still the way to go?” Scott Stanford didn’t hesitate. “No.”

His view was blunt: the traditional forty-year corporate career is fading out. He put it into perspective in a way that made the room understand this historical shift. A banker’s month’s worth of work — the decks, the models, the analysis — roughly equates to about 1,000,000 OpenAI tokens. In pure cost terms, that is about two dollars worth of AI compute. Let that sink in.

AI will not erase analyst classes tomorrow, but it will shrink them. Headcount drops. Efficiency rises. He even told his daughter, a senior at Wharton, not to pursue investment banking and instead pushed her toward careers built on real human interaction and charisma — the edge AI cannot fully copy. At least for now.

The Bull Case

With the fear above, you might picture a future like the movie WALL-E: human innovation leading to our own obsolescence. While that may be one long-term possibility, the bull case Michael and Scott presented is more optimistic and pragmatic.

As enterprises pursue efficiency and reduce headcount, millions of small- and medium-sized businesses (SMBs) could emerge as the economic solution. Think of the vibrancy of a farmers market or an emerging-market street economy: people creating small businesses that capture value in niches that AI and large firms overlook.

If people become more entrepreneurial and creative, and if AI becomes a tool for individual business owners rather than just enterprise automation, we may see a shift toward a business-based economy. Every person can become a builder — from someone selling strawberry jam at a market to a creator running an AI-driven service. That distributed creativity could be the growth engine that replaces lost white-collar consumption.

The Real Moat

As much of this piece has been written through a negative lens, I wanted to end on a note of optimism and share the advice Michael and Scott offered for young people trying to navigate what comes next.

To thrive in an AI-shaped workforce, the most valuable people will be those who are AI native, creative, charismatic, and analytical.

Simply knowing how to use AI as a companion already gives you an advantage over many peers. Pair that ability with creativity, the capacity to win people over, entrepreneurial thinking, and genuine conviction behind your decisions, and you have a combination that helps you stand out.

As one speaker put it: “Get off the trail. Do not simply follow what worked yesterday.” The people who take calculated risks tend to win big. Playing it safe rarely leads to extraordinary outcomes. Now more than ever, many of the traditional paths that once promised stability seem to lead toward a future that feels far less certain than it once did.

Tinker

One of the first things Michael Jones asked the room was, “How many of you consider yourselves AI native?” About 60 percent raised their hands. Then he followed up: “How many of you actually have AI agents running in the background right now?”

Both speakers agreed: AI is not just a helpful assistant. It is coming for the workforce. The primary debate is timing. Like every major technological shift, the only moat is proximity to the tools.

Their advice was simple: tinker. Solve silly problems. Make an agent that auto-swipes on Tinder. Build a voice AI that calls your mom for a daily check-in. It sounds stupid until it is not. The point is not the project — it’s familiarity with the tools. If you are not experimenting, you are behind. Staying ahead of the curve is not optional; it is the only way to remain valuable in a world where intelligence is becoming cheap.

The Founder Mindset

Although many of the traits below are more commonly associated with founders, Michael and Scott argued they apply to anyone who wants to succeed in the future. They suggested many successful founders share a similar personality profile: intense narcissism, near-psychopathic conviction, a belief they cannot lose, and — interestingly — many did not come from wealthy backgrounds.

The first three traits are, to some extent, within your control. Relentless self-improvement and extreme optimism can look irrational, but many of history’s most consequential builders were far from ordinary. They believed in themselves long before the world gave them reason to.

Both speakers also noted that founders without a fail-safe or safety net are often the ones who succeed. The pressure to win, and the knowledge that failure is not an option, creates a hunger that comfort and security rarely produce.

“Be Really Nice to Your Parents”

Scott preempted the inevitable question about advice for the younger generation with a comical yet cynical line: “Be really nice to your parents.” The joke drew laughs, but it landed with an uncomfortable sense of realism.

Large corporations like Oracle, Amazon, and Box have announced waves of layoffs, and the long-term stability of many white-collar roles feels increasingly uncertain. Whether you interpret that as doomerism or realism depends on your perspective — but one thing is clear: the shift is already underway.

What you do about it is the important question. Learn the tools. Build real human skills. Take calculated risks. And, perhaps, be a little kinder to your parents along the way.
