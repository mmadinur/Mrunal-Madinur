ICONIQ Reflection

I want to take a moment to reflect on what I genuinely believe will be one of the most defining experiences of my life: my internship at ICONIQ Growth.

It might sound dramatic, however, the level of exposure I had was unlike anything I had ever experienced before. The volume of information, the sheer credibility of my coworkers, and the caliber of conversations recalibrated my understanding of what “high level” actually means. Yes, my skill set improved in very tangible ways (Excel mouse trap). But the real shift was not technical. It was mental.

There were moments when I would sit in a room and almost laugh at how unreal it felt — across the table from partners behind Anthropic’s Series F, on calls with billionaires, and in conversations with former leaders of companies like Adyen, Datadog, and Coupa. All while working alongside some of the most driven individuals I have ever met.

For my Suits lovers, below is the absolute greatest way I can depict my time at ICONIQ.

Takeaways

1. Ethos cannot be overstated

Coming from a state school, I used to hear the same recycled line all the time: school does not matter, only hard work does. This is entirely incorrect.

Background and credibility shape first impressions, and those impressions determine which rooms you enter.

Before you even open your mouth, people are sizing you up. Where you went to school is one of the first filters. It is a quick and efficient way for others to gauge your discipline, your standards, and the level of competition you were able to survive. Fair or not, that is how the world works.

2. Reputation is priceless

Credibility matters, but underperforming is the quickest way to become forgettable. Ethos gets you in the room. Results determine whether you deserve to stay.

Pedigree alone has a short shelf life. It may earn you an introduction, but it does not excuse complacency.

At ICONIQ, nearly everyone came from a top school or elite bank. That background was not a safety net rather an expectation. No one relaxed because of where they came from. If anything, the pressure to uphold that standard was even higher.

3. Ego is fuel

In a world this competitive, there is no room for insecurity or self doubt. No one is slowing down so you can decide whether you belong. Whether it is objectively true or not, you have to believe you are the best person for the job. Conviction is table stakes.

If you hesitate, someone else will not. The gap is rarely intelligence, it is belief. Confidence in your judgment and the willingness to stand by your decisions is what separates you.

The world does not reward the best Excel or PowerPoint monkeys. It rewards people with a thesis. The ones who can process information, form clear opinions, and defend them without flinching. That is where winning lives.

4. Never be so stubborn you cannot change your mind

This is a slight variation of Tim Cook’s quote, “Never be so proud you can’t change your mind,” but the principle is the same. Extreme confidence is powerful, yet it can quietly evolve into a know it all mindset. Conviction and presence matter. However, the ability to identify your blind spots and actively learn from others is an even stronger signal.

This becomes especially clear in investing. When you are taking hundreds of calls a month, you are constantly exposed to founders who live and breathe their markets. You cannot afford to enter every conversation assuming you are the smartest person in the room. The real skill is being able to contribute meaningfully to high level discussions while staying humble enough to absorb new information. That balance is rare.

One of my biggest takeaways was simple: everyone has something to teach. The moment you think you have nothing left to learn is the moment you stop growing.

5. Take every call

As mentioned above, everyone has something to teach. Whether it is one of the hundred founder calls you take as an investor or a conversation with your five year old cousin, there is always something to learn. Knowledge is not scarce. It is everywhere, if you are paying attention.

Because industries run so deep and expertise compounds over years, the easiest way to accelerate your learning is simple: talk to the people who are already great at what you are trying to understand. Go straight to the source. Professionals compress decades of trial and error into a single conversation. If you are willing to ask thoughtful questions and actually listen, you can borrow experience instead of waiting to earn it the hard way.

6. The greatest asset will always be your network

If there is one lesson that stands above the rest, it is this: your network is everything. It shapes your opportunities, the deals you win, and the rooms you are invited into. In nearly every meaningful outcome, people are the variable.

Reaching out to those who inspire you matters, but staying in touch is what actually separates you. Anyone can make an introduction. Few people build real, lasting relationships.

An investor at ICONIQ once told me she won a competitive healthcare deal because she remembered the founder loved grape juice. Before the final meeting, she sent a small gift basket with his favorite bottle. It was simple, thoughtful, and intentional. That extra step closed the deal.

Investor Perspectives on AI and the Future of Work

Recently, I was fortunate enough to attend a USC Marshall School of Business speaker panel featuring Michael Jones and Scott Stanford.

For a quick introduction: Michael Jones is the former CEO of MySpace and the co-founder and partner at Science, a consumer-focused venture capital firm. He has backed well known consumer brands such as Dollar Shave Club and Liquid Death since inception.

Scott Stanford is the former Head of Internet Banking at Goldman Sachs and the co-founder and partner at Acme Capital, a deep-tech–oriented venture capital firm.

Having already been fascinated by the recent wave of “doomerism” articles, sitting in that room didn’t just provide insight. It heightened my genuine fear of the future.

The seminar, which revolved almost entirely around AI, opened my eyes to the socially bearish perspective of artificial intelligence. Below are my (slightly disgruntled) key takeaways.

The Bear Case

Although I’ve titled this “The Bear Case,” the speakers’ tone made what I am about to say feel less dramatic and more like reality.

Artificial intelligence will reshape nearly every industry.

We are already experiencing this shift. Log into Bloomberg and you see AI summaries. Log into Handshake and it is AI optimized job search. Glassdoor now has AI encapsulations of job reviews. Everything is being altered by AI, and it will continue to be.

Michael and Scott mentioned the Box CEO recently laying off half of his headcount. They also noted that several well known enterprises are already debating cutting headcount due to overhiring, and that is not even because of AI yet. Imagine the toll the economy will take when AI driven layoffs fully come into play.

As AI continues to be invested in and onboarded, headcount will continue to be reduced. White collar roles will be the first to go. The real fear behind this is simple. If the majority of white collar occupations vanish, incomes decline and the backbone of the capitalist economy, which is consumption, weakens.

Although they did not touch on the after effects of such an event such as market equilibrium, prices dropping, government intervention, protests, and more, I think the above is already enough. It is not exaggerated. It is a very realistic case.

Traditional Careers Are No Longer Safe

At one point, someone in the audience asked the question everyone tiptoes around: “Is investment banking still the way to go?”

Scott Stanford didn’t hesitate. “No.”

His view is simple. The traditional forty year corporate career is fading out.

He put it into perspective in a way that made the room understand this historical shift. A banker’s month’s worth of work, the decks, the models, the analysis, roughly equates to about 1,000,000 OpenAI tokens. In pure cost terms, that is about two dollars worth of AI compute. Let that sink in.

AI will not completely erase analyst classes tomorrow. But it will shrink them. Headcount drops. Efficiency rises.

He even told his daughter, a senior at Wharton, not to pursue investment banking. Instead, he pushed her toward careers built on real human interaction and charisma. The only edge AI cannot fully copy.

At least for now.

The Bull Case

With the fear that may have spread to you from above, many of you may think that Michael and Scott predict the future to resemble something like the movie Wall-E. Human innovation eventually leading to our own obsolescence.

While that may be true far into the future, the bull case is this: in the future, millions and millions of SMBs may be the solution to AI driven headcount reductions.

If you have ever been to an emerging country or even a local farmers market, that may be the closest way to visualize how I interpreted this message.

As AI continues to increase efficiency, enterprises will continue to drive down headcount spending and pour more into AI implementation. The real fear here is the decline in the job market and the lack of consumption from the largest spenders in our economy, white collar workers.

However, both speakers predicted that the bull case of AI is that people learn to become more entrepreneurial and creative, and the world shifts toward a business based economy. A world where everyone owns a business and is implementing AI agents in order to make a living.

Similar to how someone might sell homemade strawberry jam at a farmers market or run a hot dog stand outside of a concert, everyday people may become more creative and build AI driven SMBs that drive a new phase of economic growth.

The Real Moat

As much of this piece has been written through a negative lens, I wanted to end on a note of optimism and share some of the advice Michael and Scott offered for young people trying to navigate what comes next.

To thrive in an AI shaped workforce, the most valuable people will be those who are AI native, creative, charismatic, and analytical.

Simply knowing how to use AI as a companion already gives you an advantage over many of your peers. But when that ability is paired with creativity, the ability to win people over, entrepreneurial thinking, and real conviction behind your decisions, it becomes a powerful combination that helps you stand out.

As one speaker put it, “Get off the trail.” “Do not simply follow what worked yesterday.”

The people who take calculated risks are the ones who tend to win big. Playing it safe rarely leads to extraordinary outcomes. Now more than ever, many of the traditional paths that once promised stability seem to lead toward a future that feels far less certain than it once did.

Tinker

One of the first things the former MySpace CEO, Michael Jones, asked the room was, “How many of you consider yourselves AI native?”

About 60 percent of the audience raised their hands.

Then he followed up with, “How many of you actually have AI agents running in the background right now?”

Both speakers more or less agreed on the big picture. AI is not just a helpful assistant. It is not here to politely boost productivity. It is coming for the workforce. The only real debate is timing.

Like every major technological shift, the only moat is proximity to the tools. Their word was simple: tinker. Solve silly problems. Make an agent that auto swipes on Tinder. Build a voice AI that calls your mom for your daily check in. It sounds stupid until it is not.

The point is not the project. The point is understanding these tools and building familiarity.

If you are not experimenting, you are behind. Staying ahead of the curve is not optional. It is the only way to remain value-added in a world where intelligence is becoming cheap.

The Founder Mindset

Although many of the traits I am about to describe are more commonly associated with founders, I believe they apply to anyone who wants to succeed in the future.

Michael and Scott argued that many successful founders share a similar personality profile. They tend to be narcissistic, psychopathic, convinced they cannot lose, and interestingly, many do not come from wealthy backgrounds.

The first three traits are largely within your control. Being relentlessly focused on improving yourself and maintaining extreme optimism can appear almost irrational to others. But if you look at many of the great figures in business, technology, and history, they were never ordinary people. They believed in themselves long before the world had a reason to.

Both also noted that founders without a fail safe or safety net are often the ones who succeed. The pressure to win and the knowledge that failure is not really an option creates a level of hunger that is difficult to replicate when comfort and security are already guaranteed.

“Be really nice to your parents”

Scott preempted the inevitable question about advice for the younger generation. With a comical yet strangely cynical tone, he said, verbatim, “Be really nice to your parents.”

What was clearly meant to lighten the mood still landed in the room with an uncomfortable sense of realism.

As mentioned earlier, large corporations like Oracle, Amazon, and Box have already announced waves of layoffs, and the long term stability of white collar employment is becoming increasingly uncertain.

Whether you interpret that as doomerism or simply reality depends on your perspective. But one thing is clear.

The shift is already underway.
